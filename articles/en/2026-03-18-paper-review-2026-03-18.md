#### Paper 1: AI Can Learn Scientific Taste

- **Authors & Affiliation**: Jingqi Tong, Mingzhe Li, et al. (Fudan University, OpenMOSS Project)
- **Overview**:

Excellent scientists possess strong judgment and foresight, closely related to a capability called "scientific taste" – the ability to judge and propose research ideas with high potential impact.

However, prior research on AI for scientists has focused on improving execution capabilities, leaving the enhancement of scientific taste unexplored. This paper proposes:

A training paradigm called "Reinforcement Learning from Community Feedback (RLCF)" that utilizes large-scale community signals as supervision, formulating the learning of scientific taste as a preference modeling and alignment problem.

- **Proposed Method**:

RLCF leverages large-scale community signals as supervision and formulates scientific taste learning as a preference modeling and alignment problem. To enable this, it constructs "SciJudgeBench," a large-scale benchmark comprising 696,758 field- and time-matched paper pairs derived from 2.1 million arXiv papers published up to 2024.

The system consists of two models:

The Scientific Judge is a generative reward model that predicts which paper in a pair is more likely to have higher impact. The Scientific Thinker is a policy model that proposes follow-up research ideas with higher potential impact.

- **Key Results**:

Experiments show that the Scientific Judge surpasses state-of-the-art LLMs like GPT-5.2 and Gemini 3 Pro, generalizing to future year tests, unseen fields, and peer review preferences. Furthermore, the Scientific Thinker proposes research ideas with higher potential impact than baselines. This finding demonstrates that AI can learn scientific taste, marking a significant step towards achieving human-level AI scientists.

Specifically, the evaluation was conducted on a benchmark of 696,758 preference pairs and approximately 1.4 million unique papers, assessed in four settings: in-domain, temporal OOD (future year papers), metric OOD (ICLR reviews), and domain OOD (bioRxiv biology papers).

- **Significance and Limitations**:

The most significant contribution of this research is granting AI the ability to judge research quality by utilizing citation counts as objective "community feedback." This could assist in identifying truly important research from the vast sea of papers and suggest topics for researchers to pursue next. However, citation counts do not always perfectly align with scientific value (e.g., trend effects, self-citation), and citation data is scarce in emerging fields. Furthermore, truly groundbreaking scientific breakthroughs often transcend existing frameworks, and it remains unknown whether models trained on past data can predict them.

#### Paper 2: PhysMoDPO: Physically-Plausible Humanoid Motion with Preference Optimization

- **Authors & Affiliation**: Yangsong Zhang, Anujith Muraleedharan, Rikhat Akizhanov (Research institution details not explicitly stated in search results, but published on alphaXiv)
- **Overview**:

PhysMoDPO is a framework that refines text-conditioned diffusion models to generate physically plausible humanoid motions directly executable by robots. By integrating a Whole-Body Controller (WBC) into an iterative Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) pipeline, it enables zero-shot transfer of human-like motions to real robot platforms while maintaining fidelity to text and spatial commands.

Traditional motion generation models often produce motions that look natural but are not executable in physics simulators or on real robots, posing a challenge to bridge this "sim-to-real gap."

- **Proposed Method**: The core of PhysMoDPO is the incorporation of a Whole-Body Controller (WBC) into a preference learning loop. Specifically, it involves an iterative process: ① a diffusion model generates motion from a text prompt, ② the WBC evaluates the physical feasibility of that motion, and ③ feasible motions are treated as "preferred" while infeasible ones are "dispreferred," iteratively refining the diffusion model with DPO. This enables the generation of motions that satisfy both the naturalness learned from human motion datasets and the physical consistency required in robotics.

- **Key Results**: While detailed quantitative benchmark scores are not included in the search results, it is reported that:

Zero-shot transfer to actual robot platforms is achieved, generating human-like motions while maintaining fidelity to text and spatial commands. This signifies the achievement of both "visual naturalness" and "physical executability," which have been difficult for conventional motion generation methods. It is particularly groundbreaking in the field of humanoid robotics, allowing complex whole-body motions (walking, reaching, manipulation, etc.) to be handled within a unified framework.

- **Significance and Limitations**:

This research is a significant milestone in the fusion of generative AI and robotics. Enabling text-to-motion conversion can democratize robot programming, allowing robots to be instructed with complex motions without specialized expertise. However, the iterative process of DPO can be computationally expensive, and convergence may be difficult depending on the complexity of the motions and environmental conditions. Furthermore, generalization to novel motions outside the range of the training data remains a future challenge.

#### Paper 3: SaPaVe: Towards Active Perception and Manipulation in Vision-Language-Action Models for Robotics

- **Authors & Affiliation**: Mengzhen Liu, Enshen Zhou, Cheng Chi, Yi Han, Shanyu Rong, Liming Chen, Pengwei Wang, Zhongyuan Wang, Shanghang Zhang
- **Overview**:

SaPaVe is a research effort towards active perception and manipulation in Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models for robotics, accepted to CVPR 2026.

Traditional VLA models make decisions based on observations from a fixed viewpoint. However, real-world robot manipulation critically requires active camera control to observe objects from optimal viewpoints. SaPaVe addresses this by learning perception (where to look) and manipulation (what to do) in an integrated manner.

- **Proposed Method**: The core of SaPaVe lies in its "decoupled yet collaborative" training strategy for perception and manipulation.

To support this framework, it introduces "ActiveViewPose-200K," a dataset of 200,000 image-language-camera pose pairs for semantic camera motion learning, and a 3D geometric perception module to improve execution robustness under dynamic viewpoints. It also presents "ActiveManip-Bench," the first benchmark for evaluating active manipulation beyond fixed-viewpoint settings.

The model learns a sequence of processes: identifying task-relevant regions from visual input, controlling the camera to acquire better viewpoints, and planning manipulation actions from those viewpoints.

- **Key Results**:

Extensive experiments in both simulation and real-world environments show that SaPaVe outperforms recent VLA models like GR00T N1 and π_0, achieving up to a 31.25% higher success rate in real-world tasks.

This demonstrates that active viewpoint control significantly improves performance compared to fixed viewpoints. The effect of active viewpoint adjustment was particularly pronounced in environments with occlusions and tasks requiring fine manipulation (assembly, precise grasping, etc.).

- **Significance and Limitations**:

This research addresses the fundamental problem of integrating "seeing" and "acting" in robot manipulation. By implementing the human-like behavior of "changing pose to see better" in robots, the success rate in complex real-world tasks is significantly improved. However, simultaneously optimizing camera control and manipulation actions is computationally expensive, and latency can be an issue in applications requiring real-time performance. Furthermore, the quality and quantity of training data significantly impact performance, making data collection across diverse environments a future challenge.

#### Paper 4: ScienceClaw + Infinite: A Framework for Autonomous Scientific Investigation

- **Authors & Affiliation**: LAMM (MIT Laboratory for Atomistic and Molecular Mechanics)
- **Overview**:

ScienceClaw + Infinite is a framework for autonomous scientific investigation where independent agents conduct research without central coordination, and any contributor can deploy new agents into a shared ecosystem.

Unlike traditional AI research support tools, this system aims to execute the entire research process (hypothesis generation, experimental design, execution, data analysis, paper writing) without human intervention.

- **Proposed Method**:

Autonomous mutation layers actively prune an expanding artifact DAG (Directed Acyclic Graph) to resolve competing or redundant workflows, and persistent memory allows agents to continuously build complex cognitive states across multiple cycles. Infinite converts these outputs into an auditable scientific record through structured posts, provenance views, and machine-readable discourse relations, with community feedback guiding subsequent investigation cycles.

Each agent possesses specific scientific capabilities (e.g., molecular dynamics simulation, machine learning model training, literature review) and collaborates with others to advance research.

- **Key Results**:

Across four autonomous investigations – peptide design for the somatostatin receptor SSTR2, screening lightweight impact-resistant ceramics, cross-domain resonance bridging biology, materials, and music, and constructing formal analogies between urban morphology and grain boundary evolution – the framework demonstrated heterogeneous tool-chaining, emergent convergence among independently operating agents, and traceable reasoning from raw computation to published discovery.

These are all instances where the system autonomously pursued research from human-set initial conditions and generated new scientific knowledge.

- **Significance and Limitations**:

This research is an ambitious attempt towards realizing "AI scientists." As research process automation advances, human scientists can focus on creative hypothesis generation and strategic research direction, freeing them from routine tasks. Furthermore, AI agents operating 24/7 can significantly accelerate research speed. However, significant challenges remain for full autonomy: ① generation of truly innovative ideas, ② deep interpretation of experimental results, ③ ethical judgment, and ④ understanding the social context of research. There are also risks of pursuing incorrect research directions or drawing erroneous conclusions without validation.
