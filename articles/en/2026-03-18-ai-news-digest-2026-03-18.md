## Japanese AI Regulatory Trends

### Japan's AI Promotion Act Establishes "Innovation-First" Model

In a landmark move, Japan's National Diet approved the "Act on Promoting the Research, Development, and Utilization of AI-Related Technologies" (AI Promotion Act) on May 28, 2025, making Japan the second major economy in the Asia-Pacific region to enact comprehensive AI legislation.

Japan enacted the AI Promotion Act in May 2025, adopting a light-touch regulatory approach that encourages companies to cooperate with government safety measures and grants the government the power to publicize the names of companies that use AI for human rights abuses.

**Contrast with the EU:**

The EU has adopted a comprehensive, binding framework through the EU AI Act. Centered around a risk-based classification system, it imposes broad ex-ante obligations on providers and deployers of high-risk AI systems, including governance requirements, technical documentation, conformity assessments, and significant enforcement risks.

Comparing Japanese and EU policies, the most distinct difference is the question of whether to regulate AI comprehensively. Unlike the EU, Japan takes an approach of updating existing regulations within each sector. A more detailed analysis reveals two essential distinctions: (1) whether regulations pertain to AI systems interfering with human judgment or emotions, and (2) whether there are legal obligations for specific AI governance processes. Regarding (1), the EU AI Act treats applications involving interference with human judgment or internal states—such as performance evaluations, credit scoring, and emotion recognition—as high-risk applications, imposing additional regulations. In contrast, Japan handles these issues within the scope of existing labor or financial regulations and does not impose special obligations on AI.

**Enforcement Mechanisms:**

Japan relies entirely on voluntary cooperation and reputational mechanisms, penalizing or regulating rights infringements due to the improper use of AI within existing legal frameworks. The EU enforces mandatory compliance under the EU AI Act with significant penalties for violations, including fines of up to €35 million or 7% of global turnover for breaches of prohibited practices.

**International Positioning:**

Amidst these dynamic shifts in global AI policy, Japan's position remains consistent. Under the slogan of becoming "the world's most AI-friendly country," Japan's approach implements policies that maximally support AI utilization based on existing legal frameworks, incorporating agile, multi-stakeholder processes. This approach was materialized in the AI Promotion Act passed in May 2025 and fully enacted on September 1. Furthermore, regulatory reforms are rapidly advancing in many sectors to ensure existing rules do not become a burden to AI development and implementation.
